The forward search group

The core of the Forward Search group comes both from the the members of the division of statistics and computing of the University of Parma and the Department of Statistics of the London School of Economics (from now on ParmaLSE) made up of Anthony Atkinson (LSE), Marco Riani and Andrea Cerioli (Parma) . This has resulted in the publication of two books with Springer-Verlag New York (Atkinson and Riani, 2000; Atkinson Riani and Cerioli, 2004) and about 15 papers in prestigious international journals.

The last 15 years of joint research of people from ParmaLSE can be summarized as follows:

1994-2002. Introduction of the new approach. At that time we were completely unaware of many of the potentialities of the new approach and we did not think of it as a new philosophy. The emphasis was as a new technique of exploratory data analysis in linear and nonlinear regression and generalized linear models. In the case of a single population and contaminated data, the new approach revealed itself as a powerful tool for detecting the presence of outliers, both individual and grouped, and to evaluate their effect on the results of traditional analyses (Riani and Atkinson, 2000).

2002-2004. Extension of this technique to the analysis of multivariate data based on a combination of statistical modelling and graphical diagnostics. 

2005-2007. In this period we began to introduce the Forward Search not only as an exploratory tool but also for inferential purposes and we have developed a theory for the construction of robust confidence bands for the curves which are monitored along the search.

From 2008. In recent times the team has oriented its activities as follows.

  • Recently some members of the ParmaLSE team laid the foundation for the automatic application of the procedure in multivariate analysis (Riani et al. 2009). Clearly, the automation of the procedure is necessary in concrete applications where the Forward Search has to be repeated many times. One of the objectives of the team is to extend this automated procedure to multiple regression or, more generally, to the analysis of complex data. The first step in this direction can be found in Riani et al. (2008).
  • In 2008 ParmaLSE initiated a close collaboration with the European Commission's Joint Research Centre in order to understand how the new philosophy could be extended to the data of international trade and applied to related antifraud problems. The persons involved in this project at the JRC were Domenico Perrotta, Francesca Torti (now working for the University of Parma), Vytis Kopustinskas and the head of the group, Spyros Arsenis. The FSDA toolbox is one of the main outcomes of this collaboration. There is a manifest intention for the long term consolidation of our current collaboration.
  • In 2009 fruitful contacts have been established between the ParmaLSE team and the University of Rome La Sapienza (Pierluigi Conti and Alessio Farcomeni) in view of grounding the Forward Search in the theory of empirical processes. The objective is to find the limiting trajectories to which the curves monitored in the Forward Search might converge.
  • The number of PhD students who are attracted by the new philosophy is increasing. We mention two whose studies are in progress in the Parma team, in topics such as "The forward search in Bayesian statistics (Aldo Corbellini)" and "Robust classification and mixtures (Gianluca Morelli)". The intention to extend the cooperation with young researchers is also in the plans of the JRC and the wider Forward Search team. Other members of ParmaLSE (Grossi and Laurini, 2009) are working on the extension of the new philosophy to the analysis of correlated observations and more specifically to the context of extreme value theory. Unfortunately the different contributions have been proposed without a unifying scheme and appropriate coordination. The team has become aware that this is a wider problem and that more critical mass is necessary to reach a wider popularity among data analysts.

We are convinced that the lack of good statistical software is a main factor limiting the diffusion of the new philosophy and we hope that the FSDA toolbox will fill the gap. Another effort in this direction was made in 2008 by a JRC grantholder, Tomas Demcenko, who implemented SAS IML macros for the Forward Search in regression. Previously, excellent implementations in Splus and R were developed by Dr. Konis for the analysis of multivariate data (e.g. the Rfwdmd software downloadable from the CRAN site). Unfortunately, lack of resources prevented the maintenance of such implementations.

It is worthwhile to mention other people who share the same philosophy, who have been in contact from time to time with the “ParmaLSE team” and who have already given important theoretical and applied contributions to the forward search literature. For example, Fabio Crosilla of the University of Udine has extended the forward routines to data on photogrammetry. This extension has considerably improved the classification of spatial objects (Crosilla et al., 2007). Nadia Solaro is currently working to extend this new philosophy to multilevel models (Solaro and Pagani, 2009). Daniela Calò has investigated the use of forward search routines for finding groups of homogeneous observations in the context of robust cluster analysis (Calò, 2008). Silvia Salini has recently worked on robust extensions of calibration models using forward search tools (Salini, 2006). Matilde Bini has used the forward search to develop robust transformations in percentage data (Bini and Bertaccini, 2007) while Bruno Bertaccini, together with Roberta Varriale, has extended the new philosophy to the robust analysis of variance (Bertaccini and Varriale, 2007).

The forward search philosophy of data analysis presents strong connection with other approaches of robust statistics. For example an iterative procedure, closely related to the forward search, is considered in García-Escudero and Gordaliza (2007) that may be applied in robust heterogeneous clustering problems. We also require interaction with researchers who use approaches based on dynamic models which show strong points of contact with our philosophy (A. Monti). For example, the group intends to investigate the use of the robust adaptive estimates proposed in Di Ciccio and Monti (2004, 2008) and their integration into our philosophy of data analysis. Another contact point with the robust literature is linked to weighted likelihood (Markatou, Basu and Lindsay, 1998) and minimum distance methods, where outliers and inliers are defined according to a probabilistic scale. This scale is based on the comparison between the empirical distribution function and that of the assumed model. The advantages of using definitions of outliers and inliers based on a probabilistic scale, rather than on a geometric one, are several. Here we just mention direct applicability in a wide set of models including circular data (Agostinelli, 2007), mixture models (Markatou, 2000) and generalized linear models (Agostinelli and Al-quallaf, 2009).

In summary, it is true to say that the growth of the Forward Search method into a philosophy and practical approach of data analysis is largely due to the initiative and leadership of Anthony Atkinson, Marco Riani and Andrea Cerioli who, when the method was introduced, had the capacity to move the research well beyond the then understood scientific boundaries. However, we also believe that the success of this philosophy of data analysis relies on a wider combination of circumstances and people, which comprises these ingredients:

  • Cultivate and create space for fresh ideas. Young researchers at doctoral and post-doctoral level will be always more than welcome. New mathematical results providing theoretical underpinnings to the observed excellent performance of our methodology of data analysis (Stochastic processes group).
  • A stable practical laboratory for the assessment of our theoretical foundations, providing a considerable number of applications to real data affected by different sources of heterogeneity (JRC group). We are firmly convinced that only a close interaction between theoretical understanding and practical exploration can ensure advancement towards our objectives.
  • Professionally written open source software. A Software group should be created and extended beyond the current FSDA developers.
  • Spread the new methodology to different fields of application and at the same time extend the procedure theoretically and empirically to the new problems which arise from the different contexts (Statistics and engineering group).
  • Interact with scientists from the robust statistics community in order to understand how some recent proposals (i.e. those based on robust adaptive estimators and the weighted likelihood approach) can be integrated into our new philosophy of data analysis (Robust statistics group).
